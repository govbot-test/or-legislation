SUMMARY
The following summary is not prepared by the sponsors of the measure and is not a part of the body thereof subject
to consideration by the Legislative Assembly. It is an editor’s brief statement of the essential features of the
measure. The statement includes a measure digest written in compliance with applicable readability standards.
Digest: The Act changes the law about compensation for wrongful convictions and makes a new
PCR process when a conviction is based on some discredited science. The Act goes into effect when
the Governor signs it. (Flesch Readability Score: 60.9).
Modifies provisions relating to petitions for compensation for wrongful conviction.
Creates a new post-conviction relief petition process when a person has a conviction that is
based on scientific expert testimony, scientific expert evidence or scientific expert opinion derived
from specified discredited forensic science disciplines. Sunsets the new petition process on January
2, 2031.
Declares an emergency, effective on passage.

43 SECTION 6. (1) A person convicted of a crime may file a petition for post-conviction re-
44 lief if the conviction was based in whole or in part on scientific expert testimony, scientific
45 expert evidence or scientific expert opinion derived from the application of one or more of
[6]
B-Eng. SB 1515
1 the following discredited forensic science disciplines:
2 (a) Hair microscopy;
3 (b) Bite mark analysis or bite mark comparison; or
4 (c) Comparative bullet lead analysis.
5 (2) Notwithstanding ORS 138.530, in a post-conviction relief proceeding based on a petition
6 described in this section, the court shall grant relief if the petitioner proves, by a prepon-
7 derance of the evidence:
8 (a) That the petitioner’s conviction was based substantially on scientific expert testi-
9 mony, scientific expert evidence or scientific expert opinion that applied one or more of the
10 discredited forensic science disciplines described in subsection (1) of this section; and
11 (b)(A) If the petitioner was convicted at trial, that there is a reasonable probability that
12 had the scientific expert testimony, scientific expert evidence or scientific expert opinion
13 applying the discredited forensic science discipline not been admitted at trial, the outcome
14 of the trial would have been different; or
15 (B) If the petitioner was convicted after pleading guilty or no contest:
16 (i) That the scientific expert testimony, scientific expert evidence or scientific expert
17 opinion applying one or more of the discredited forensic science disciplines was known to the
18 petitioner at the time of entering the plea; and
19 (ii) That the scientific expert testimony, scientific expert evidence or scientific expert
20 opinion was a material factor in the petitioner’s decision to plead guilty or no contest.
21 (3) When making a determination under subsection (2) of this section, the court shall
22 follow the procedures described in ORS 138.620.
23 (4) If court finds that the petitioner has met the requirements of subsection (2) of this
24 section, the court shall grant appropriate relief under ORS 138.520.
25 (5) ORS 138.550 (3) and (4) do not apply to petitions for post-conviction relief described
26 in this section.
27 (6) A person may file a petition under this section notwithstanding the fact that:
28 (a) The person did not object to the admission of the scientific expert testimony, scien-
29 tific expert evidence or scientific expert opinion applying the discredited forensic science
30 discipline at trial or raise the issue on appeal, unless the trial occurred on or after the ef-
31 fective date of this 2026 Act;
32 (b) The person pleaded guilty or no contest to the conviction;
33 (c) Before or after the conviction, the person made a confession or admission; or
34 (d) The person has completed the sentence associated with the conviction.
35 (7) This section shall be liberally construed to promote justice and to correct wrongful
36 convictions based on discredited forensic science disciplines.
37 (8) This section may not be construed to permit challenges to convictions for which the
38 forensic scientific evidence introduced at trial was based on objective and validated compu-
39 tational methods that have replaced the subjective visual comparison techniques used in the
40 discredited forensic science disciplines described in subsection (1) of this section.
41 (9) As used in this section:
42 (a)(A) “Bite mark analysis” means the diagnosis of an injury as a human bite mark.
43 (B) “Bite mark analysis” does not include making an identification based on DNA analy-
44 sis of any biological material that may be present within or around a bite mark or suspected
45 bite mark.

to consideration by the Legislative Assembly. It is an editor’s brief statement of the essential features of the

Digest: The Act changes the law about compensation for wrongful convictions and makes a new
PCR process when a conviction is based on some discredited science. The Act goes into effect when
the Governor signs it. (Flesch Readability Score: 60.9).
Modifies provisions relating to petitions for compensation for wrongful conviction.
Creates a new post-conviction relief petition process when a person has a conviction that is
based on scientific expert testimony, scientific expert evidence or scientific expert opinion derived
from specified discredited forensic science disciplines. Sunsets the new petition process on January
2, 2031.
